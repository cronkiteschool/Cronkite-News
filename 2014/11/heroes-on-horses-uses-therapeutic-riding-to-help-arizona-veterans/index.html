Heroes on Horses uses therapeutic riding to help Arizona veterans

Tweet

Thursday, Nov. 6, 2014
 By Imelda Mejia
Cronkite News

TUCSON – Joseph Bailey Jr. starts his Thursday mornings with Fiesta, a black-and-white-patterned horse. Bailey brushes Fiesta and whispers in his ear for him to behave before the two begin their ride.

A Navy veteran, Bailey lost the use of his left leg and left hand due to a spinal problem that developed three decades after his discharge. The Southern Arizona VA Health Care System referred Bailey to Heroes on Horses to help him rehabilitate from a 2002 spinal cord surgery.

“I now can do a lot more than I had before,” he said. “A lot of it due to that I am out here doing this type of therapy that causes me to use the functions of my leg.” listen

Therapeutic Riding of Tucson offers Heroes on Horses free of charge to Bailey and other veterans through donations from the community.

Melanie Roeder, Bailey’s therapeutic riding instructor, said the experience is more than treatment.

“When people are coming here to ride, they’re not coming to therapy, they’re coming to horseback ride,” she said. listen

Bailey, who had no experience with horses before coming here, said he’s learned they need to be spoken to as if they are people.

“They have personalities just like people, unfortunately,” he said. “They are not people, but still you have to give them the same respect.” listen

The sessions, offered for 12 weeks in the fall and spring, are tailored to meet the veterans’ individual goals. However, every rider practices stretching and mounting the horse.

Therapeutic Riding of Tucson has special lifts for those who are wheelchair-bound, and Bailey uses an ankle-foot brace as he rides so he can control his leg more while connecting with Fiesta.

“We work well together, said Bailey, who worked as a legal secretary in the Navy. “I thank him when I get off, I rub him, and then we put him away. It’s a great relationship to have.” listen

Army veteran James Nalin completed two tours in Iraq before he was involved in a truck rollover accident back in the U.S. that injured his hip, causing him to have balance problems. In the same year, he was diagnosed with traumatic brain injury. He said Heroes on Horses has helped him heal emotionally as well as physically.

“There was a lot of trust issues, because you couldn’t trust anybody over there,” he said. “And then coming back here, working with the horse has helped me build trust with the horse. And by me building a trust with the horse has helped me build trust with other people.” listen

Nalin said that without the program he would probably be isolated right now, as he was for the five years after leaving the Army.

“That’s how bad I was when I came back. I didn’t go anywhere, I didn’t do anything,” he said. “And then I got involved with this program, and now I’ve done more. I’ve started getting back into the community to do stuff again.” listen

Mary Vardi, program director for Therapeutic Riding of Tucson, started Heroes on Horses in 2006 to help treat veterans facing spinal cord injuries, traumatic brain injuries, post-traumatic stress disorder and the aftermath of a stroke or amputation.

Veterans have a certified therapeutic instructor with them as they practice exercises targeted at control and balance. One exercise involves the veteran practicing an up-and-down motion while the horse walks. The exercise, called posting, targets the rider’s thigh muscles.

Although Vardi is the program director for Therapeutic Riding of Tucson, she still instructs for the Heroes on Horses program.

“It’s really important to me,” Vardi said. “It’s a part of kind of who I am, bringing independence to a special-needs rider.” listen

The Southern Arizona VA Health Care System connects veterans with the program because it offers them a chance to build camaraderie and rehabilitate their bodies.

“We always keep it in the back of our minds as a venue that we can use,” Susan Lucht, clinical director for the system’s polytrauma center. “Mary Vardi understand veterans, and she has been a great support for our veterans.” listen

Former Navy aircraft mechanic Francisco Rene Suarez was one of the first to join the program eight years ago and stayed with it after achieving his goal of becoming an independent rider. He said he even finds it hard to stay away from the ranch when sessions are on break.

“I want to go see my other horse buddies, you know,” Suarez said. “I just want to go down and say, ‘Hi.’ So I still do, I come down.” listen
