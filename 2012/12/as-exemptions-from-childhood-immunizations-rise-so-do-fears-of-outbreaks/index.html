Outbreak fears rise with exemptions from childhood immunizations

Tweet

Monday, Dec. 17, 2012
 By Danielle Verbrigghe
Cronkite News

PHOENIX – The percentage of Arizona parents citing personal beliefs in opting out of vaccines required for children has increased steadily since 2000 yet remains relatively low overall: 3.4 percent among kindergartners during the 2011-2012 school year, for example.

But Will Humble, director of the Arizona Department of Health Services, looks with worry at other numbers detailing immunizations against diseases such as measles, mumps, whooping cough and chickenpox. Those are ZIP codes around the state with much higher percentages of parents opting out.

In schools and communities within those ZIP codes, he said, diseases largely unseen today in the U.S. could circulate easily.

“There are ZIP codes in Sedona and other places where there is a real risk of a very severe outbreak of a lethal disease,” Humble said. “And since it hasn’t happened yet, they see it as theoretical. But if you ask me it’s a matter of time.”

While most states allow parents to opt out of vaccinations for medical or religious reasons, Arizona is among a minority that allow exemptions for philosophical or personal reasons. A state law allows parents with children in schools, preschools and child care centers to submit a personal beliefs exemption without consulting a medical professional.

In interviews with Cronkite News, experts and advocates said the fear of side effects, belief that certain diseases aren’t harmful and desire to live a natural lifestyle contribute to the rise in personal beliefs exemptions, as does some parents finding it more convenient not to vaccinate.

“Arizona has been very open to giving parents choices in these matters,” said Rep. Debbie McCune Davis, D-Phoenix, program director for the Arizona Partnership for Immunization. “Where it is becoming a concern is the increase in the use of exemptions.”

Arizona requires those entering kindergarten to be vaccinated against diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, polio, measles, mumps, rubella, hepatitis B and chickenpox. In later grades they must receive boosters for diphtheria, tetanus and whooping cough as well as the vaccination against meningococcal disease.

The personal beliefs exemption allowed by the state requires parents to sign a form acknowledging that they understand the risks of not vaccinating and that unvaccinated children could be excluded from school if an outbreak occurs.

In the 2011-2012 school year 3.4 percent of kindergarteners had personal beliefs exemptions for at least one required vaccine. For the 2000-2001 school year that figure was 1.4 percent.

State and county health officials say that beyond those numbers are areas and schools with much higher opt-out rates and as a result lower immunity and greater risk of disease outbreaks.

In Yavapai County, 9.2 percent of kindergartners, 9.4 percent of sixth-graders and 11.5 percent of 10th-graders had personal beliefs exemptions on file for the 2011-2012 school year. Yavapai County health officials said rates were especially high in Sedona, Cornville and Oak Creek as well as Prescott.

In Coconino County, 6.2 percent of kindergartners, 11.6 percent of sixth-graders and 4.1 percent of 10th-graders had personal belief exemptions.

Dr. Karen Lewis, medical director for the Immunization Program Office at the Arizona Department of Health Services, said that clusters of higher exemption rates and the lower immunity levels that result put schools and communities at risk.

“People of like belief tend to hang out with each other,” Lewis said. “So you are more likely to have contact with other people who are not immunized, and you can have a rapid spread of a very serious illness quite quickly.”

McCune Davis helped sponsor a bill during the 2012 legislative session that would have required parents submitting personal beliefs exemptions to first talk with a health professional. It wasn’t taken up in committee.

She said the change could reduce exemptions based on convenience or bad information that stokes fears among parents.

“I think that they are being used for convenience instead of being used the way they are intended,” McCune Davis said.

Dangers

Leslie Maier’s son, Chris, scored the winning goal to lead Tucson’s Sabino High School to the 2005 Division 4A state soccer championship. Two weeks later, he got a headache.

He went to soccer practice, but the coach sent him home. That night he started vomiting. The next day, suffering chills and saying he was unable to feel his feet, he was in the hospital.

Less than 24 hours after his first symptom, Chris Maier died of meningococcal meningitis, a disease that today can be prevented by a vaccine.

Shortly after her son’s death, Leslie Maier learned that the Food and Drug Administration had approved a meningococcal disease vaccine for adolescents. She helped lead the campaign for Arizona to require it, which happened in 2008.

“I don’t think that all parents realize that there are diseases so deadly as meningitis that could take their healthy child in less than 24 hours,” she said.

Maier said that if Chris had lived he would have likely lost his arms and legs and been brain damaged. She hopes that by educating other parents about the risks she can prevent other kids from getting sick.

“I think when kids get vaccinated, at least against meningitis, they get saved from horrible side effects, if not death, from getting that disease,” she said.

Other diseases prevented by vaccines, including measles and whooping cough, can be fatal.

Even chickenpox, which many parents think of as minor, can be very serious, said Shoana Anderson, office chief for the Office of Infectious Disease Services at the Arizona Department of Health Services.

“People forget that people die of chickenpox,” Anderson said. “When you’ve seen a hundred people survive it seems like one death is a rare event, but when that one death happens to you it’s very tragic.”

Experts say vaccines also protect others who can’t be immunized because they are too young, such as newborns, or have medical problems. Higher vaccination rates create what’s known as herd immunity, a level of immunity in the population that makes a disease unlikely to spread.

Machrina Leach, nurse program manager for Maricopa County Department of Public Health, said diseases like measles and pertussis, also known as whooping cough, can be especially dangerous for infants and those with weakened immune systems.

One baby in Maricopa County died this year of whooping cough,  the most common vaccine-preventable disease in Arizona, and Leach said that death could have been prevented if everyone around the baby had been immunized.

“With herd immunity, the more people you have immunized, then it can’t go anywhere, but the less people you get immunized, the more it will spread,” Leach said.

Whooping cough has been on the rise over the past several years, with 901 cases across the state in 2012 through Dec. 10. Although rare, measles, mumps, tetanus, meningococcal meningitis and other diseases prevented by vaccines surface periodically in Arizona.

Dr. Karen Lewis said that parents should consider the risks before not vaccinating children.

“I understand that they have a right to choose not to have their children vaccinated, but the rest of us have a right not to be exposed to vaccine-preventable diseases,” she said.

Even diseases like polio that have been nearly eradicated in the United States pose threats, Lewis said.

“We work vigorously to educate the public about the importance of vaccination, that all of these deadly diseases, even though you don’t see them very often in this country, they are only a plane ride away from anywhere else in the world,” she said.

Relative risks

A vaccine, like any other medication, isn’t completely risk-free, but side effects from vaccines are rare, according to Jane Seward, deputy director of the Division of Viral Diseases at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.

“Nothing is without risk,” Seward said. “Crossing the street is risky. You could have an allergic reaction to a shot of anything.”

The most common side effects of vaccine shots are a sore arm, redness at the injection site and fever. Very rarely, vaccines can trigger severe effects like a high fever, fibrile seizure or symptoms mimicking the disease itself, such as a rash, Seward said.

Glen Nowak, CDC senior adviser to the director for the National Center for Immunization and Respiratory Diseases, said that parents often perceive the likelihood of a severe reaction as higher than it really is.

“We often think that if a child gets an illness, there are drugs and medical interventions that will be successful and can be implemented for vaccine-preventable disease,” he said. “It turns out the reason vaccines are so important is these are very hard-to-treat diseases.”

Dr. Steve Mousser, pediatric department chairman at Mercy Gilbert Medical Center, said that vaccine-preventable diseases are likely to be far more harmful than side effects from vaccines.

“When you look at the risk of an adverse event (from a vaccine) and the risk of an adverse outcome as a result of one of the illnesses, the risk of the illness far exceeds the risk of the vaccine,” he said.

Health officials say many parents choose not to vaccinate because of unfounded concerns, such as the fear that the measles-mumps-rubella vaccine causes autism or that too many vaccines at once can overwhelm a child’s immune system.

Patrice Sparks, public health nurse supervisor for Yavapai County Community Health Services, said that while some parents worry that too many vaccines administered at once will overwhelm their child’s immune system, babies can produce antibodies for about 100,000 different vaccines at one time.

“The antibodies that we’re producing when we’re little are huge. We’re just making them to all kinds of things,” Sparks said.

Another common concern, a perceived link between the MMR vaccine and autism, stems from a widely discredited study by an English doctor. Public health officials and medical professionals said that there is no credible evidence to support this link.

Yet Sparks said many parents continue to fear vaccines might cause autism.

“That’s a myth I’m afraid we’re never going to be able to dispel,” Sparks said. “It was such bad science.”

Rochelle Myers of Phoenix said she got all vaccinations for her two older daughters but delayed getting the MMR vaccine for her 5-year-old son because she began hearing reports of a link to autism. But she changed her mind when he started school, saying the risks of the diseases outweighed her uncertainty about the vaccine.

“I just don’t want my children getting sick because I didn’t take them to get vaccinated,” she said.

Parents’ concerns

Prescott mom Anne Legge chose to get some vaccines for her 6-year-old twins but opted out of the vaccine for measles, mumps and rubella and the varicella vaccine, which protects against chickenpox.

“I think that there’s certain vaccines that I will absolutely give to the kids,” she said. “But there’s also vaccines that I think it’s healthy for the immune system to get these illnesses and work from there.”

Legge said she put a lot of thought into her decisions about vaccines. She ruled out MMR because of a manufacturer warning for families with a history of autoimmune disease. She would consider immunizing her children against chickenpox only if they don’t get the disease by the time they are teenagers.

She said it’s important for parents to have the right to choose what is ultimately best for their own children.

“If anybody ever had concerns about what we choose to do with our children, I would say, ‘Well, it puts my child at risk to do MMR, so therefore I’m going to do what’s in the best interest of my children,’” Legge said.

Julie Trevizo, an administrative specialist in communicable disease for Yavapai County Community Health Services, sets up appointments for other families to get immunizations but, unlike most of her colleagues, doesn’t believe vaccines are necessary.

“I wasn’t a vaccinated child. Neither were my siblings,” she said.

Trevizo didn’t vaccinate her two oldest children, now 28 and 30. Her two younger children, ages 14 and 15, have gotten the combination vaccine that protects against tetanus, diphtheria and whooping cough and the human papillomavirus (HPV) vaccine, which Arizona doesn’t require. She used exemptions to opt out of other vaccines.

She said she hears the argument that not vaccinating puts the community at risk every day at her job and sees a community need to vaccinate in instances such as a smallpox outbreak, but not in the case of diseases such as measles, mumps or rubella.

“I think it’s one of your own rights as a parent,” Trevizo said. “I don’t think that anybody should be able to make the decision for you.”

Experts say parents who choose to opt out or delay vaccines for personal beliefs do so for a wide range of reasons.

University of Arizona Researchers, including Kacey Ernst, an assistant professor at University of Arizona’s College of Public Health, analyzed state data, surveyed parents and doctors and held town halls for a study the Arizona Department of Health Services commissioned on personal beliefs exemptions.

The first part of the study, released Dec. 13, showed schools with higher rates of exemptions tended to have higher percentages of white students and lower rates of free and reduced lunch. Charter schools and schools in the northern part of Arizona also tended to have higher rates of exemptions, the study showed.

“The personal belief exemptions are much less about people not being able to access vaccines and much more about they don’t want vaccines for their child,” Ernst said.

Ernst said that some parents are concerned about the side effects of vaccines, including the discredited autism link. Others worried children were getting too many vaccines at once. Some parents favored a natural lifestyle, she said, preferring natural exposure to disease to immunization.

On the other hand, many parents who opted out didn’t believe the risks of disease were severe, Ernst said.

Dr. Karen Lewis said that many parents don’t see diseases like whooping cough, polio, meningitis and measles as risks because immunization has been so successful at preventing outbreaks.

“It’s very difficult because the people who don’t want to be immunized or have their children immunized are frightened,” she said. “It’s an emotional reaction. You can tell them the statistics and they just haven’t seen the diseases.”

Lewis said that lower-income parents tend to have a positive attitude about vaccines.

“Whereas the upper socioeconomic people with higher education, in many studies, actually are the ones that have more vaccine refusal,” Lewis said.

David McAtee, spokesman for Yavapai County Community Health Services, said officials there worry that some parents get exemptions simply for convenience.

“We just want to make sure that it’s not just a convenience thing and that you’re not putting your child’s health and the health of all the other children in the school at risk because you just can’t take the time,” he said.

The future

The Arizona chapter of the American Academy of Pediatrics plans to advocate again during the 2013 legislative session for a law requiring parents seeking vaccination exemptions to be counseled by a health professional on the risks.

Sue Braga, the group’s executive director, said such a requirement would address problems with parents getting exemptions for the sake of convenience or because of bad information, while still upholding the right of parents to make their own decisions.

“It involves education, getting rid of any misinformation,” she said. “Hopefully we can start raising the herd immunity levels so we’re not compromising other people in the community.”

Debbie McCune Davis, the Democratic state lawmaker and immunization advocate, said Washington implemented a similar requirement and has seen lower exemption rates as a result.

“When a health care provider has the opportunity to meet with a parent and talk with them not only about the risks but the benefits of vaccines, the parent makes better choices,” she said. “The decision that comes is better informed.”

Will Humble, the state Department of Health Services director said he’ll evaluate results of the University of Arizona’s study on exemptions before considering next steps. But he said the decision to vaccinate affects more than just the child.

“It’s a community responsibility,” Humble said. “If you don’t vaccinate your kids it’s not just that you’re putting your kids at risk, you’re putting the community at risk.”
